\begin{frame}{Economics and Public Policy Research}
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Mattias Polborn (1998) - \textit{Mandatory Insurance and the Judgment-Proof Problem}
            \begin{itemize}
                \item Mandatory financial assurance or insurance can force firms to internalize risk \textit{ex-ante}.
            \end{itemize}
        \item Lucija Muehlenbachs (2015) - \textit{A Dynamic Model of Cleanup: Estimating Sunk Costs in Oil and Gas Production}
        \begin{itemize}
            \item Shows that firms use temporary closure as a way to avoid the high up-front costs of plugging
        \end{itemize}
        \item Judsom Boomhower (2019) - \textit{Drilling Like There's No Tomorrow: Bankruptcy, Insurance, and Environmental Risk}
        \begin{itemize}
            \item Bonding improves environmental outcomes by reducing moral hazard associated with judgement-proofness.
        \end{itemize}
    \end{itemize}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{What does good policy look like?}
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Good policy thinks of bonding as an \textit{insurance/actuarial} problem. There are small probability events that are very costly to the state.

        \item Good policy incentivizes operators to plug wells that have reached the end of their productive life rather than punt on the high up-front costs.
    \end{itemize}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Conclusions}
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Utah has an opportunity to reduce the risks posed by the current stock of aging wells.
        \item Good policy
        \begin{itemize}
            \item matches risk with bonding amounts.
            \item incentivizes plugging to reduce risk.
        \end{itemize}
        \item The current regulation has lots of design features that match good policy.      
    \end{itemize}
\end{frame}
